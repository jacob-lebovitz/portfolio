Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Across these visualizations, a consistent pattern emerges: while non-investors account for the majority of transactions by volume, investors, particularly institutional actors, command a disproportionate share of transaction value and appear increasingly present over time. All transaction activity, specifically investor activity, is concentrated in urban areas around the center of Boston, making them interesting areas to study in regards to investor and non-investor behavior. However, we did find that some suburban areas have high investor activity, which suggests that Boston’s housing submarkets are interconnected rather than isolated. Maybe investment pressure in dense urban cores may extend outward into lower-density neighborhoods. At the same time, new construction appears more accessible to non-investors than older housing stock, raising important planning implications. Overall, this dataset provides rich information around the housing transaction markets in Boston. However, outliers and heavy skews in the data can make it difficult to create interpretable results over all of the data. Going forward, it is important to look at zip codes individually, as well as separating investor and non-investor activities by proportion to account for bias in the data quantities.
